About Me

My name is Aidan Sawyer. I'm a Web Developer, Brewer, Distiller, bartender, and fireman with a great appreciation for simplicity, interoperability, and separation of concerns.

I think everything is better crafted when done both with an earnest interest/investment in its purpose and from within a pretty deep knowledge of the context for its use. I try to hold myself to these measures throughout my life.

Software is a powerful tool for communicating and facilitating ideas and supporting work across any number of industries. While it's not my only calling and interest, it's a means of expression and a way to dabble and explore interesting ideas, support my other work (and future family), and to maintain my skills over time.

I spent much of my early career working at positions in and around academic research and libraries. We live in a world replete with information and media in any number of fields, and finding ways to organize, access, enjoy, parse, and discuss them remains a challenging and important problem for our age. I retain much reverence for the goals and principles at the foundation of Library and Information Sciences, but ended up finding the practical inner-workings of institutional libraries rather lackluster and ended up pursuing other avenues.

I've spent the last couple years exploring emergency response, writing, hiking/orienteering, and in craft beverage creation for what they provide in space and time and human experience. I intend to get back into software for my previous interest in cataloguing and preservation as well as craft beverage production, and potentially emergency response. Time will tell where all I'll go.

How I Work

When working with software, I tend to seek, wherever possible, to push the boundaries of what can be done with the web platform itself, without defaulting to large frameworks-of-the-day, overly-elaborate and complicated build systems, extraneous libraries, and even vanilla javascript or CSS.

There are many guiding design principles in support of why I do this, but the most directly applicable term I've found so far is called the principle of least power. It basically says, don't use a fancy, new sledge-hammer when a trusty, regular old handheld one will do the job.

I follow this principle for a number of reasons:

  • I want to learn the web itself, not a fashionable (but fleeting) abstraction on top of it
  • I want the capabilities of the web platform to grow and compound over time
  • I want my work to be more performant, accessible, and interoperable
  • I want the skills that I learn to remain useful over my entire career
  • I want to ensure (for myself and for others) that my work lasts (doesn't break)
  • I want my want the projects I work on to be easy to maintain, update, and predict
  • I find it challenging and engaging to test the capacities of the base layer, to see what it can already do

Esoteric References and Complaints About the Librarianship and Software Industries

I'll permit myself a self-indulgent diversion, here. I left working for institutions for a variety of reasons related to a lack of fidelity to what I viewed as their main calling and purpose. It was always meant to be temporary, but I became interested in other things along the way. I should write about this at some point at greater depth, but here are some breadcrumbs for you or I to follow through on (or not) later.

The software industry spends billions of dollars chasing money and building tchotchkes for ad revenue and click-bait at the shortest time-scale possible. University Libraries shovel tens of millions (each) outsourcing away their content, metadata, and expertise, then buying back temporary access to it with little knowledge of how it's used.

We're sitting on the greatest invention for storing and accessing human knowledge ever invented. It's sad we're selling that for pottage, and I didn't want to watch from so close anymore. The web deserves a better class of stewards.

Anyway, Tim Berners Lee has some super interesting ideas about how the internet could have gone if universities and research institutions had retained proper interest in the project they started.

Stewart Brand and the Long Now Foundation have some really fantastic work that both mainstream librarianship and web development could learn a whole hell of a lot from and which should be informing "our" work from the very begining. He introduces the concept of Pace Layers which work very closely into some of the rationale for why I work how I do and why I stepped away.

Ted Nelson's "Project Xanadu" is an incredibly beautiful and deeply fascinating take on a vastly different way the internet could have gone. His ideas are certainly worth (more than) an afternoon of exploring.
